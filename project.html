monster

As much I wanted it to be, Monster isn't your typical teen flick, trust me cause its hits d i f f e r e n t. It's slow-burn, sure, but the way it builds tension is unreal. You start with this single mom, worried about her son after a fight at school, and then BAM, everything spirals. drive my car

Man, the "drive" part in Drive My Car is way more than just cruising down the highway.

It's a metaphor, see? (shoutout to the director, Ryusuke-sensei and Murakami-sensei). The main character, this actor dude named Yusuke (played by the incredible Hidetoshi Nishijima), is super messed up after his wife does some spoiler-ly thingies. After those thingies, He's directing a play in Hiroshima, and because of, well, stuff, he needs a driver. Enter Misaki (played by Toko Miura, one of the vocalist from one of my other favorite movie).

They spend most of the movie together, you know, driving around Hiroshima. But it's not just sightseeing. They talk, they listen, they share their burdens. It's like being stuck in traffic with your best friend, only way deeper and more meaningful. They don't just zoom past the scenery; they use the drives to navigate their own emotional landscapes, you know? It's a slow process, like a long, winding road, but by the end, they both reach a kind of acceptance, a way to move forward despite the baggage they carry. Kinda like how we all gotta figure out how to deal with our own emotional baggage, right? So yeah, Drive My Car might be a slow burn, but the metaphorical drive is what makes it such a powerful journey to survive in this sorrow-ridden worlds.
